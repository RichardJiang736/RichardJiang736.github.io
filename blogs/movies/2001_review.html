A Prologue

“Standing on the shoulders of giants.” No other sci-fi film has ever been as respected and revered as 2001: A Space Odyssey. Yet as we immerse ourselves in its imaginative and realistic world, I can’t help but notice that certain aspects are often neglected when analyzing this film.

Vision and Patience...

The common focus tends to be on its cinematography and its wildly psychedelic, abstract imagery. But I am, as usual, more of a storytelling-centric reviewer. This film holds a special place in my heart because of how its story is told—and more specifically, because of its pacing.

It’s no wonder 2001 is often ranked among the most “boring” films of all time. I’ll admit, somewhat sheepishly, that I’ve dozed off during parts of it on more than one viewing. This feels strange and contradictory, given everything I know about the film. On one hand, it paints a brilliant vision of the future and a chilling warning about unrestrained technological advancement. On the other hand, its hypnotic pacing seems to push the audience away from fully engaging with those ideas. It’s like a car with a beautifully designed exterior, explosive imaginative flourishes, and top-notch build quality—only to discover that it was never meant to be driven, but merely admired as a conceptual inspiration of what cars *could* be.

When I’ve discussed this with friends, I’ve been surprised to hear the opposite reaction. The pacing didn’t bother them at all; in fact, they accepted it as part of the art. For them, the imagery and the pacing worked together, each enhancing the other. I had considered that perspective before voicing my complaint, but I still believe there are better ways to expand upon this story than by forcing the audience to simply accept the pacing as it is. After all, isn’t the point of a great work of art that it should be both enjoyable and thought-provoking? If people can’t enjoy it in the first place, then no matter how far the imagination stretches, can it really be called great?
