lincoln

spielberg. 2012.

introduction

Throughout history, we've seen gods of war and conquerors—figures we admired but could never truly follow. Individuals with immense power who could have done good, yet chose otherwise. Especially in times of crisis, who would want a leader who stumbles from defeat to defeat, only to respond not with humility or growth, but with fury—casting blame on the people in a desperate attempt to preserve the illusion of control? That illusion, once intoxicating, becomes a poison: dragging him deeper into denial, until he's consumed by the void and swallowed by the very hell he helped create.

Lincoln, by contrast, offers a portrait of leadership not built on invincibility, but on endurance—where moral clarity and quiet resolve eclipse the spectacle of power, and where the burden of history is shouldered, not wielded.

in the lens of storytellers

In general, film reviewers opt to analyse the key technical features employed by the film to gain a lucid understanding of how the story unfolds. But in the case of Spielberg films, and this film in particular, the way the story was told is arguably more important.

Take it this way. Across human culture, how were stories generally passed down? A language with a sophisticated writing system, that's easy. But for most languages, stories were passed down orally. Nevertheless, whether written down or spoken, a good screenplay functions to deliver both memorably. And to document the historical event of passing the 13th Amendment to abolish slavery—led by arguably the most widely celebrated president in US history—was an immense and difficult task. That's why for almost a century no one dared to make a film on Lincoln—at least successfully—precisely because of the immense responsibility entailed. Till Tony Kushner came along and wrote this screenplay.

Perseverance, diligence, courage, and most of all—having the right heart in mind. These are traits that Lincoln embodied. His perseverance was firmly established in scene 3, where despite Mr and Mrs. Jolly expressing their negative view towards passing the amendment if not for the cause of ending the Civil War but to abolish slavery, he continued to push forward and hold onto his moral beliefs.

His diligence surfaces across the film, but one moment stands out most vividly: Lincoln spending the night in the War Cabinet with two telegraph operators, personally overseeing the message to Grant long after others had retired.

And through it all runs the thread of his heart. Most powerfully in the scene set in Lincoln's Office: "Not only of the millions now in bondage but of unborn millions to come."

in the lens of artists

cinematography, production and sound

Spielberg opted to shoot in 35mm film, a practical and appropriate choice. It's not a film focused on visual spectacles—it's about delivering performance and telling a story. It's a perfect blend between dramaticism and realism.

In most scenes, Lincoln is framed in a deliberately composed medium close-up—an ostensibly neutral vantage that subtly amplifies his physical bearing and posture. This shot choice invites the viewer into Lincoln's embodied experience, allowing us to inhabit the weight of his deliberations while maintaining spatial awareness of the surrounding tensions.

Elsewhere, the film expands its visual grammar to evoke a more layered cinematic texture. Wide shots assert Lincoln's stature within the physical and political architecture he navigates, rendering his silhouette as both solitary and emblematic.

In contrast, close-ups pierce the surface, excavating micro-expressions and tonal shifts that reveal the emotional granularity beneath his composed exterior. Together, these shot variations construct a portrait that is both monumental and minutely human.

Everything—from the set to the costumes down to the sound of Lincoln's watch—was tuned to maximum historical precision. Sets were built as replicas of the original in order to achieve that exact feel of the mid-19th century atmosphere.

daniel day-lewis

Daniel Day-Lewis is a living portrait of the president himself. Spielberg reportedly missed his performance the moment the actor returned to being himself, so striking was his portrayal. His devotion—a near year of research and preparation for this single role—to capturing Lincoln's essence is shown in all its glory. He embodies Lincoln perfectly: tender and soulful, convincingly weary and stoop-shouldered.

thaddeus stevens

Under the shadow, one who advocates for the abolishment of slavery equally as Lincoln—Thaddeus Stevens. Ruthless in achieving his cause. Unforgiving in confronting his opponents. Precisely due to his persistence and determination, he helped Lincoln to successfully achieve a cause that would otherwise be impossible. He is, at least in my heart and mind, the true Lincoln's right hand man, even though William Seward took the pleasure to support and counsel the president's decisions. And of course, all of this was delivered and portrayed by Tommy Lee Jones' astonishing performance.

favourite moments

lincoln's stories

Lincoln loves to tell little stories. In times of crisis, these stories serve as critical momentum for those around him while easing his opponents into conformity. My favourite one has to be his explanation on Euclid's Axioms, where "things that are equal to the same thing are always equal to each other." Lincoln gently explained this to an operator, subtly reminding him that the path to knowledge is always open; the real question is whether one chooses to walk it.

debate unleashed in the house of representatives

The scenes in the House of Representatives were a full on dramatic masterpiece. One after the other, from democrats to republicans, each representative articulates his thoughts in rhetoric that breathes authority, humanity, and wisdom. Stevens unleashed his memorable smack down on Pendleton and Wood.
